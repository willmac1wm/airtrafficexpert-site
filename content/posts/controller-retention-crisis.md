I recently had conversations with three experienced controllers—each with over a decade of service—who are actively looking for exits from the profession. Their stories aren't unique. Across the country, facilities are losing experienced controllers faster than they can train replacements. This isn't just a staffing problem; it's a retention crisis that threatens the stability of our air traffic system.

## The Numbers Tell a Story

The FAA's controller workforce is aging, with a significant percentage eligible for retirement in the next 5-10 years. But here's what's more alarming: we're not just losing controllers at retirement age. We're losing controllers in their 30s and 40s—people who should be at the peak of their careers—who are leaving for opportunities outside ATC.

When you lose a controller with 10-15 years of experience, you're not just losing one person. You're losing:

- **Institutional knowledge** that takes years to acquire
- **Training capacity** (experienced controllers train new ones)
- **Operational flexibility** (they can work the most complex traffic)
- **Mentorship** for the next generation

## Why Are They Leaving?

After talking with dozens of controllers over the years, several themes emerge:

### Compensation Doesn't Reflect Reality

Let me share a personal example: As an ATC instructor under the SAIC federal contract, I make $35/hour. Meanwhile, I know controllers who've left for positions at airlines, private airports, or aviation companies making 50-100% more. The message this sends is clear: your experience and expertise aren't valued enough to compete with other aviation opportunities.

The issue isn't that controllers are underpaid in absolute terms—it's that the compensation doesn't align with:

- **Years of training required** (typically 2-3 years before full certification)
- **Level of responsibility** (literally holding lives in your hands)
- **Stress and pressure** of the job
- **Opportunity cost** of choosing ATC over other aviation careers

### Work-Life Balance Challenges

ATC scheduling is notoriously difficult:

- **Rotating shifts**: Including overnight shifts that disrupt sleep patterns
- **Mandatory overtime**: Common in understaffed facilities
- **Weekend and holiday work**: Missing family events becomes routine
- **Schedule unpredictability**: Difficulty planning personal life

While some of this is inherent to 24/7 operations, understaffing makes it worse. When you're perpetually short-handed, the burden falls disproportionately on those who remain.

### Geographic Inflexibility

The current system often requires multiple moves throughout a controller's career:

- **Initial assignment**: Often not where you want to be
- **Career progression**: Moving to busier facilities for advancement
- **Facility closures/downsizing**: Forced relocations
- **Family considerations**: Spouse careers, kids' schools, aging parents

For controllers who've established roots—bought homes, had kids, have working spouses—the requirement to move becomes a deal-breaker.

### Limited Career Progression

In many facilities, there's a ceiling:

- **Supervisory positions are limited**: Not everyone can (or wants to) move into management
- **Pay caps exist**: At some point, experience doesn't translate to higher pay
- **Recognition is limited**: Outstanding performance isn't always rewarded
- **Alternative paths are scarce**: Few options beyond traditional ATC roles

Talented, ambitious controllers look around and ask: "Where do I go from here?" When the answer is "nowhere," they look elsewhere.

### The Stress Factor

ATC is inherently stressful, but several factors make it worse:

- **Chronic understaffing**: Doing the work of multiple people
- **Inadequate support**: Both from management and peer support systems
- **Public scrutiny**: When things go wrong, controllers bear the blame
- **Lack of control**: Over schedules, assignments, facility decisions

Controllers can handle stress—it's part of the job. But unsupported, chronic stress leads to burnout.

## What Needs to Change

### Immediate Actions

**1. Compensation Reform**
Create pay structures that actually compete with other aviation careers. This includes:

- Base pay increases that reflect responsibility and experience
- Bonuses for retention milestones
- Competitive benefits packages
- Recognition programs with financial rewards

**2. Staffing Investment**
Hire aggressively and train effectively to reduce overtime burden on existing controllers. This requires:

- Expanded training programs
- Better instructor compensation (to retain instructors)
- Streamlined hiring processes
- Investment in training infrastructure

**3. Schedule Flexibility**
Where operationally feasible, offer:

- More predictable schedules
- Self-scheduling options
- Compressed work weeks
- Better work-life balance programs

### Long-term Solutions

**1. Career Path Diversity**
Create alternative career progressions:

- Technical specialist roles (not just management)
- Instructor positions with adequate compensation
- Consulting and advisory roles
- Industry liaison positions

**2. Geographic Flexibility**
Reduce forced relocations:

- More local hiring and development
- Transfer options rather than requirements
- Work-from-home for appropriate positions (like instructors)
- Retention incentives for hard-to-staff locations

**3. Wellness Programs**
Invest in controller well-being:

- Mental health resources
- Stress management training
- Peer support programs
- Physical fitness programs

**4. Recognition and Value**
Make controllers feel valued:

- Public recognition of excellent performance
- Input into operational decisions
- Professional development opportunities
- Career advancement that rewards excellence

## The Cost of Inaction

Failing to address retention has cascading consequences:

- **Safety risks**: Tired, overworked, understaffed facilities
- **Service degradation**: Delays, restrictions, reduced capacity
- **Training challenges**: Can't properly train new controllers when short-staffed
- **Organizational knowledge loss**: Decades of experience walking out the door
- **Reputation damage**: Makes recruiting harder

## A Call to Action

This isn't a problem that fixes itself. It requires:

- **Leadership commitment**: From FAA administration to facility management
- **Adequate funding**: From Congress and appropriations
- **Industry collaboration**: Airlines, airports, and other stakeholders
- **Controller input**: Those doing the job know what needs to change

The controllers I mentioned at the beginning of this article? They didn't want to leave. They chose ATC as a career because they loved it. But when the job becomes unsustainable—when you can make significantly more money with better work-life balance elsewhere—the choice becomes obvious.

We can reverse this trend, but it requires acknowledging the problems and taking meaningful action. The question is: will we act before the system reaches a breaking point?

## What You Can Do

**If you're a controller:** Document your concerns, participate in surveys, and communicate with your union representatives.

**If you're in management:** Listen to controller feedback, advocate for your facility's needs, and support retention initiatives.

**If you're a policy maker:** Understand that this is a systems problem requiring systemic solutions, not just band-aids.

**If you're in the aviation industry:** Support efforts to improve controller compensation and working conditions—your operations depend on effective ATC.

The future of air traffic control depends on our ability to retain experienced professionals. Let's not wait until it's too late to act.

*Will Macomber is an FAA-certified Air Traffic Controller and instructor who advocates for controller compensation and retention reform.*
